fleet-scale congestion comparison

10 Robots. 1 WiFi Link.
Only MoQ Keeps Them All.

Each robot streams 10 data tracks at 38.5 Mbps — safety heartbeat, control commands, joint sensors, IMU, force/torque, point cloud, and three camera feeds. When WiFi degrades (steel hulls, welding EMI, AP congestion), total fleet demand exceeds available bandwidth. What happens to robot control depends entirely on the transport protocol.

What's Happening Under Congestion

MoQ: Priority Starvation

Each track gets a QUIC stream with explicit priority. Under congestion, the scheduler sends P255 heartbeat first, then P200 control, down to P1 video. All 10 robots keep full control. Video degrades to ~2 fps — the operator sees choppy cameras but never loses the robot.

Zenoh: Equal Degradation

Separate QUIC streams per topic — no head-of-line blocking. But without priority, the scheduler treats heartbeat and video equally. Under fleet congestion, video frames fill the write queue and heartbeat packets wait behind them. Latency exceeds the 500ms watchdog — robots intermittently lose contact.

WebRTC: Head-of-Line Blocking

All data muxed into a single ordered DTLS stream per robot. One lost video packet blocks everything behind it — including e-stop. Under fleet congestion, retransmissions cascade. Random robots freeze completely for 200-800ms at a time. The operator cannot predict which robot will respond.

Methodology: telemoq's --no-priority mode disables QUIC priority labels, producing identical scheduling behavior to Zenoh QUIC multistream. --single-stream mode muxes all tracks into one stream, matching WebRTC/DTLS behavior. All three modes run in a single Rust binary against the same MoQ relay — the only variable is the transport strategy. Benchmarks at telemoq-benchmark.html.
