Priority delivers 4.2x more control data than no-priority, and 95x more than single-stream. The robot stays under control with MoQ. With WebRTC-style transport, it goes dark.
Setup: 8 tracks at ~3 MB/s aggregate — exceeds QUIC connection capacity, forcing congestion. BBR limits throughput; the scheduler chooses what to deliver.

Three modes: default = per-stream priority (P0–P20) • --no-priority = all priority 0 (DDS-like) • --single-stream = one QUIC stream (WebRTC-like HOL blocking)

Transport architecture determines whether your robot stays under control.

Priority: P0–P5 get 3–5 msgs/s each. Video/pointcloud starved to zero. Total control: 22.2 msgs/s.
No-priority: Delivery chaotic. Which tracks survive is arbitrary. Total control: 5.3 msgs/s — 76% loss.
Single-stream: Near-total blackout. Total control: 0.2 msgs/s — 99% loss.

What this means for teleop safety

Priority (MoQ)

Heartbeat: 3.0/s. Joints: 3.7/s. Force/torque: 3.7/s. Control streaming: 4.0/s. Video and pointcloud sacrificed — exactly as designed. The robot stays under control.

"9,600 bps was enough for control." — IHMC DRC

No priority (DDS-like)

Heartbeat: 2.0/s. Streaming: 0/s. IMU: 0/s. Joints: 0.4/s. 70% less control data. The scheduler doesn't know heartbeat matters more than video. Which tracks survive is a coin flip.

Single stream (WebRTC-like)

Heartbeat: 0/s. Streaming: 0.1/s. Everything else: 0/s. Complete blackout. One ordered stream means one lost packet blocks everything. The operator cannot e-stop the robot.

How it works

Priority: Each track = separate QUIC stream. stream.set_priority() → quinn's scheduler sends P0 first, P20 last.
No-priority: Separate streams, all priority 0. FIFO — no track preferred.
Single-stream: All tracks in one QUIC stream. One lost packet blocks everything (HOL blocking).
