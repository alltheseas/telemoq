Why MoQ Keeps Your Robot Under Control

Industrial WiFi in shipyards suffers 10-20% packet loss (NIST). The industry default — WebRTC — puts all streams in one ordered channel: one lost video packet stalls control data. MoQ gives each stream its own QUIC priority. Video degrades; control keeps flowing.

Delivery rates under congestion

Three modes, one binary, same relay. Under severe throttling, priority sustains ~30% for control tracks — but 30% vs. 0% is the difference between control and blackout.

Priority: 22.2/s   No-priority: 5.3/s   Single-stream: 0.2/s Control data throughput under congestion. Three modes, one binary. Priority delivers 4.2x more than DDS-like, 95x more than WebRTC-like. See full benchmark →
Key Takeaway (measured)

MoQ (priority): Safety heartbeat at 30%, control/task at 29%. Priority starvation works — video and pointcloud starved to 0% so control data survives. 22.2 control msgs/s.

No-priority (DDS-like): Only heartbeat survives at 20%. Control/streaming, joints, IMU all drop to 0%. Force/torque gets 3% randomly. 5.3 msgs/s.

Single-stream (WebRTC-like): Total blackout. Every track at ~0%. HOL blocking kills everything. 0.2 msgs/s.

What happens under 15% packet loss

click to expand

WebRTC: Head-of-Line Blocking

One lost video keyframe stalls every stream behind it — including e-stop. At 15% loss, control gaps of 200-800ms happen multiple times per second.

MoQ: Priority Scheduling

Each track gets its own QUIC stream with explicit priority. P0-P3 control sent first; P20 video starved last. The robot's 12ms forward extrapolation rides through brief gaps.

Shipyards: 10-20% Packet Loss

Steel hulls act as Faraday cages. Arc welding generates broadband EMI. NIST measures 10-20% loss in these environments. E-stop must always get through.

Built on IHMC's Lessons

click to expand

Safety Can't Wait Behind Video

E-stop must always get through. MoQ ships safety signals first — video can wait.

9,600 bps Was Enough

IHMC's Atlas operated on a 9,600 bps control link at the DRC. Control is tiny; video is the bottleneck. Sacrifice video to protect commands.

Task Goals, Not Joint Commands

Operator sends high-level goals; robot runs its own 1kHz control loop. The teleop link tolerates brief interruptions.

Teleop Today, Autonomy Tomorrow

Per-stream timestamps capture all channels in sync. Every teleop session produces training data with priority-guaranteed completeness.

Known Demo Limitations

click to expand

This demo proves priority scheduling. It does not replicate production conditions.

LimitationWhat the demo doesWhat production requires
Localhost only Pub & sub on same machine via loopback. Clocks perfectly synced. Multi-hop relay, NTP drift (1-10ms), asymmetric paths. Add ~20ms encode + ~5ms decode.
Bandwidth constraint, not random loss Bandwidth throttling triggers priority starvation. Shipyards have random packet loss (NIST: 10-20%). BBR can collapse under >5% random loss — priority scheduling doesn't help if BBR shuts down the pipe.
No real video codec 50KB zero-filled blobs at 30fps. H.264/H.265 with I-frame/P-frame dependencies. Losing an I-frame degrades more than a P-frame. No GOP structure tested.
No bidirectional flow Single publisher simulates both directions. Two independent publishers at relay. Bidirectional priority under asymmetric congestion is unsolved.
Forward extrapolation claimed, not shown Inter-arrival gaps displayed vs. 12ms KST window. Simulated robot arm extrapolating trajectory during gaps, then snapping back on data resume.
No connection migration Static QUIC session on loopback. Robot roaming between APs. QUIC connection IDs survive network change (vs. WebRTC ICE restart, TCP death).

The WiFi → Cellular Handoff Problem

click to expand
ProtocolOn network changeDowntime
TCP Connection bound to IP/port 4-tuple. IP changes → connection dies. 2-10s
WebRTC ICE restart: re-gather candidates, re-negotiate. No control during restart. 0.5-3s
DDS Multicast discovery fails on new subnet. Not designed for roaming. Varies
QUIC (MoQ) Connection ID survives IP change. PATH_CHALLENGE validates; session continues. 0-RTT
Status: QUIC connection migration is defined in RFC 9000 §9. Quinn has client-side support. Not yet implemented in moq-relay.
